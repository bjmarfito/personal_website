Getting Started with InSAR: From Raw SAR to Deformation Maps

InSAR Tutorial Sentinel-1

Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar (InSAR) is one of the most powerful tools in the modern geophysicist's kit. With free Sentinel-1 data from ESA, you can measure centimeter-scale ground deformation over hundreds of kilometers — from your laptop. This post walks through the key steps in the processing chain.

What is SAR?

Synthetic Aperture Radar is an active microwave sensor mounted on a satellite. Unlike optical cameras, SAR works day and night, through clouds, and can penetrate vegetation to some degree. It illuminates the Earth with microwave pulses and records the backscattered signal — both amplitude (how bright) and phase (where in the wave cycle the echo arrived).

The InSAR Principle

If we take two SAR images of the same area from (nearly) the same orbit position but at different times, we can subtract the phase of one from the other. Any change in the radar-to-ground distance — caused by ground deformation, for example — will show up as phase fringes in the resulting interferogram. Each full color cycle (fringe) corresponds to half the radar wavelength of line-of-sight displacement (~2.8 cm for Sentinel-1 C-band).

Key Processing Steps

  1. Co-registration — align the two SAR images to sub-pixel accuracy
  2. Interferogram formation — complex multiply image 1 by the conjugate of image 2
  3. Flat-Earth & topographic phase removal — subtract the phase due to Earth's curvature and topography (using a DEM such as SRTM or Copernicus)
  4. Filtering — reduce phase noise with an adaptive filter (e.g., Goldstein filter)
  5. Phase unwrapping — convert wrapped (−π to π) phase to continuous displacement field
  6. Geocoding — project from radar coordinates to geographic coordinates

Tools to Get Started

The easiest entry point is ESA's SNAP (free), which handles Sentinel-1 SLC data natively. For time-series analysis (tracking deformation over many acquisitions), StaMPS, MintPy, and ISCE are widely used in the research community.

Common Pitfalls

  • Temporal decorrelation — vegetation and water lose coherence quickly; prefer short time baselines in vegetated areas
  • Atmospheric noise — tropospheric water vapor causes phase delays that mimic deformation; correct using ERA5 or GACOS
  • Orbital errors — long-wavelength ramps in the interferogram from imprecise orbital data; correct by removing a polynomial trend or using precise orbits

InSAR is an art as much as a science — the best results come from understanding your specific tectonic setting and knowing which artifacts to expect. Happy interferogramming!
